1. First, find the eigenvalues of \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \) by solving \( \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \): \( \det\left(\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix}\right) = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \).
2. Verify the determinant calculation: \( (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) = 3 - \lambda - 3\lambda + \lambda^2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 \), then add 2 to get \( \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 \), confirming correctness.
3. Solve the characteristic equation \( \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \): the roots are \( \lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} = 2 \pm i \).
4. Since the eigenvalues are complex conjugates, \( C \) should have the form \( \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix} \) with \( a = 2 \), \( b = 1 \), so \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \).
5. Find an eigenvector for \( \lambda = 2 + i \) by solving \( (A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0 \): \( A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} 1-(2+i) & -2 \\ 1 & 3-(2+i) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix} \).
6. Set up the system: \( (-1-i)v_1 - 2v_2 = 0 \), \( v_1 + (1-i)v_2 = 0 \); from the first equation, \( v_1 = \frac{2}{-1-i}v_2 \).
7. Simplify \( \frac{2}{-1-i} \) by multiplying numerator and denominator by the conjugate \( -1+i \): \( \frac{2}{-1-i} = \frac{2(-1+i)}{(-1-i)(-1+i)} = \frac{2(-1+i)}{1+1} = -1 + i \).
8. Thus, an eigenvector is \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 + i \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \).
9. The real and imaginary parts of this eigenvector are \( \vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \), \( \vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \).
10. Form \( P \) using the real and imaginary parts: \( P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \).
11. Verify that \( P \) is invertible: \( \det(P) = (-1)(0) - (1)(1) = -1 \neq 0 \), so \( P \) is invertible.
12. Compute \( P^{-1} \): for \( P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-1} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \).
13. Verify \( A = P C P^{-1} \): compute \( C P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \).
14. Then \( P (C P^{-1}) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = A \), confirming correctness.
15. Final answer: \( P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \), \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \), such that \( A = P C P^{-1} \).
