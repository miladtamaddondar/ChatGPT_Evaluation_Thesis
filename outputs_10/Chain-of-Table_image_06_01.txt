1. We want to find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB = 0\), where \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\). 2. Let \(B = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 & \mathbf{b}_2 \end{bmatrix}\), where \(\mathbf{b}_1\) and \(\mathbf{b}_2\) are two different nonzero column vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^2\). 3. We need \(A\mathbf{b}_1 = 0\) and \(A\mathbf{b}_2 = 0\), so we solve \(A\mathbf{b} = 0\) for a generic vector \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix}\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3x - 6y \\ -x + 2y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\). 4. This gives the system: \(3x - 6y = 0\), \(-x + 2y = 0\). 5. Solving \(-x + 2y = 0\) gives \(x = 2y\), substitute into the first equation: \(3(2y) - 6y = 0 \implies 6y - 6y = 0\), which is always true, so the solution is all scalar multiples of \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\). 6. Therefore, any nonzero column of \(B\) must be a scalar multiple of \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\), so choose two different nonzero multiples, e.g., \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\). 7. Thus, one possible \(B\) is \(\boxed{B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}}\).
