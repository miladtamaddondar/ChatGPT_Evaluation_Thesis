1. To construct a \( 4 \times 3 \) matrix of rank 1, recall that a matrix has rank 1 if and only if all its rows (or columns) are scalar multiples of a single nonzero row (or column), so let's choose a nonzero column vector \( \mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \) and a nonzero row vector \( \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \end{pmatrix} \) and form their outer product. 2. The outer product \( \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T \) gives the matrix \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \times 5 & 1 \times 6 & 1 \times 7 \\ 2 \times 5 & 2 \times 6 & 2 \times 7 \\ 3 \times 5 & 3 \times 6 & 3 \times 7 \\ 4 \times 5 & 4 \times 6 & 4 \times 7 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 10 & 12 & 14 \\ 15 & 18 & 21 \\ 20 & 24 & 28 \end{pmatrix} \). 3. To verify the rank is 1, observe that each row is a scalar multiple of the first row: the second row is \(2\) times the first, the third is \(3\) times the first, and the fourth is \(4\) times the first, so the row space is one-dimensional. 4. Alternatively, since every \(2 \times 2\) minor (determinant of any \(2 \times 2\) submatrix) is zero (e.g., \(\det \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix} = 5 \times 12 - 6 \times 10 = 60 - 60 = 0\)), the rank cannot be greater than 1. 5. Therefore, the constructed matrix \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 10 & 12 & 14 \\ 15 & 18 & 21 \\ 20 & 24 & 28 \end{pmatrix} \) is a \( 4 \times 3 \) matrix of rank 1.
