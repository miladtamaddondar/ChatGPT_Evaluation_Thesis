We are given \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) and need to find \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T\); this means the \(2 \times 3\) matrix equals the outer product of \(\mathbf{u}\) and \(\mathbf{v}\).
2. Let \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}\), so \(\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix}\); this follows from the definition of the outer product.
3. Equate the corresponding entries: \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\), \(2v_1 = 2\), \(2v_2 = -6\), \(2v_3 = 8\); this is valid because the matrices must be equal entrywise.
4. Verify: \(2v_1 = 2 \times 1 = 2\), \(2v_2 = 2 \times (-3) = -6\), \(2v_3 = 2 \times 4 = 8\); all match the second row of the given matrix, confirming correctness.
5. Final answer: \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\).
