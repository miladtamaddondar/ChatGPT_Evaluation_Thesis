1. We are given \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) and asked to find \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T\).  
2. The outer product \(\mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T\) gives a \(2 \times 3\) matrix: \(\mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix}\).  
3. Set the corresponding entries equal: \(\begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\).  
4. Equate the first row: \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\).  
5. Equate the second row: \(2v_1 = 2\), \(2v_2 = -6\), \(2v_3 = 8\), which confirms \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\).  
6. Therefore, the solution is \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\).
