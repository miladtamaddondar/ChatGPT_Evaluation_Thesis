1. Write the vectors as columns of a matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -3 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & -4 & -3 & 1 \\ -3 & 2 & 1 & -8 & -6 \\ 2 & -3 & 6 & 7 & 9 \end{bmatrix}\) to analyze their linear independence.  
2. Use row reduction (Gaussian elimination) to row echelon form to find the pivot columns, which correspond to linearly independent vectors.  
3. Subtract 1 times column 1 from column 4 and 2 times column 1 from column 5: new columns 4 and 5 are \([0, -3, -5, 5]\) and \([0, 1, 0, 5]\) respectively.  
4. Subtract 3 times column 1 from column 3: new column 3 is \([0, -4, 10, 0]\).  
5. The updated matrix is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -4 & -3 & 1 \\ -3 & 2 & 10 & -5 & 0 \\ 2 & -3 & 0 & 5 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\); verify by back-substitution that the operations are correct.  
6. Focus on the submatrix formed by columns 2, 3, 4, 5 (since column 1 is already a pivot): \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -4 & -3 & 1 \\ 2 & 10 & -5 & 0 \\ -3 & 0 & 5 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\).  
7. Perform row operations on this submatrix to find further pivots: subtract 2 times row 1 from row 2 and add 3 times row 1 to row 3, yielding \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -4 & -3 & 1 \\ 0 & 18 & 1 & -2 \\ 0 & -12 & -4 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\).  
8. Add \(\frac{2}{3}\) times row 2 to row 3: row 3 becomes \([0, 0, -\frac{10}{3}, \frac{40}{3}]\).  
9. The pivots are in columns 1, 2, and 3, so the first three columns of the original matrix are linearly independent; verify by checking that no nontrivial linear combination of them yields zero.  
10. Therefore, a basis for the space spanned by the vectors is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 2 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ -4 \\ 1 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
