1. We are given \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) and need to find \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T\); since \(\mathbf{u}\) is \(2 \times 1\) and \(\mathbf{v}^T\) is \(1 \times 3\), their product is \(2 \times 3\), matching the left side, so the multiplication is valid.
2. Write out the product: \(\mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot v_1 & 1 \cdot v_2 & 1 \cdot v_3 \\ 2 \cdot v_1 & 2 \cdot v_2 & 2 \cdot v_3 \end{bmatrix}\); this uses the definition of the outer product.
3. Set corresponding entries equal: \(\begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix}\); this is valid since matrices are equal iff all corresponding entries are equal.
4. Equate the first row: \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\); this follows from matching the first row of both matrices.
5. Verify the second row: \(2v_1 = 2\), \(2v_2 = -6\), \(2v_3 = 8\) gives \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\), which matches the values from step 4, confirming consistency.
6. The solution is \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\).
