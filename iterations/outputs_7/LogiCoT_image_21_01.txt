2. To determine uniqueness, we check if \( A \) is invertible by computing \( \det(A) \).  
3. Calculate \( \det(A) = 1 \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 6 \\ -2 & -5 \end{vmatrix} - 0 \begin{vmatrix} -2 & 6 \\ 3 & -5 \end{vmatrix} + (-2) \begin{vmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 \end{vmatrix} \).  
4. Compute minors: \( \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 6 \\ -2 & -5 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(-5) - (6)(-2) = -5 + 12 = 7 \) and \( \begin{vmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 \end{vmatrix} = (-2)(-2) - (1)(3) = 4 - 3 = 1 \).  
5. Substitute minors: \( \det(A) = 1 \cdot 7 + 0 \cdot (\cdot) + (-2) \cdot 1 = 7 - 2 = 5 \).  
6. Since \( \det(A) \neq 0 \), \( A \) is invertible and the solution is unique.  
7. The unique solution is \( \mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b} \).  
8. To find \( \mathbf{x} \), we can use Cramer's Rule or row reduction; here, we use row reduction on the augmented matrix \( [A | \mathbf{b}] \).  
9. Write the augmented matrix: \( \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 & 7 \\ 3 & -2 & -5 & -3 \end{array}\right] \).  
10. Add 2 times row 1 to row 2: \( R_2 \to R_2 + 2R_1 \Rightarrow [0, 1, 2, 5] \).

13. Add 2 times row 2 to row 3: \( R_3 \to R_3 + 2R_2 \Rightarrow [0, 0, 5, 10] \).  
14. The matrix is now \( \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 & 10 \end{array}\right] \).  
15. Divide row 3 by 5: \( R_3 \to R_3 / 5 \Rightarrow [0, 0, 1, 2] \).  
16. Back substitute: From row 3, \( x_3 = 2 \).  
17. From row 2: \( x_2 + 2x_3 = 5 \implies x_2 + 4 = 5 \implies x_2 = 1 \).  
18. From row 1: \( x_1 - 2x_3 = -1 \implies x_1 - 4 = -1 \implies x_1 = 3 \).  
19. The unique solution is \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \).

21. Final answer: The unique vector \( \mathbf{x} \) is \( \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \).
