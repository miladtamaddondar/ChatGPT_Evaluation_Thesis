1. Recognize that $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T$ is the outer product of a $2 \times 1$ vector and a $3 \times 1$ vector, resulting in a $2 \times 3$ matrix; this matches the size of the given matrix, so the equation is well-posed.
2. Write $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1v_1 & 1v_2 & 1v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix}$, which is a $2 \times 3$ matrix as required.
3. Set up the equation $\begin{bmatrix} 1v_1 & 1v_2 & 1v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$ and equate corresponding entries to form a system of equations.
4. From the first row, equate: $1v_1 = 1$, $1v_2 = -3$, $1v_3 = 4$, so $v_1 = 1$, $v_2 = -3$, $v_3 = 4$; verify that these satisfy the first row.
5. From the second row, check: $2v_1 = 2$, $2v_2 = -6$, $2v_3 = 8$; substituting $v_1 = 1$, $v_2 = -3$, $v_3 = 4$ gives $2 \cdot 1 = 2$, $2 \cdot (-3) = -6$, $2 \cdot 4 = 8$, which matches the second row, confirming correctness.
6. Therefore, the solution is $\boxed{\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}}$.
