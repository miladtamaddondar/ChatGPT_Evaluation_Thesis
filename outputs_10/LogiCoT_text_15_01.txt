1. Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\).  
2. Solve the characteristic equation \(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\): \(\lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{(-4)^2 - 4 \cdot 1 \cdot 5}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16-20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} = 2 \pm i\).  
3. Verify the eigenvalues are complex conjugates, so \(C\) must be of the form \(\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix}\) with \(a = 2\), \(b = 1\), since eigenvalues of such a matrix are \(a \pm ib\).  
4. Find an eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2+i\) by solving \((A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0\): \(A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} 1-(2+i) & -2 \\ 1 & 3-(2+i) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}\).  
5. Set up the system: \((-1-i)x - 2y = 0\) and \(x + (1-i)y = 0\); from the first equation, \(x = -\frac{2}{-1-i}y\), rationalize denominator: \(-\frac{2}{-1-i} = \frac{2}{1+i} = \frac{2(1-i)}{(1+i)(1-i)} = \frac{2(1-i)}{2} = 1-i\), so \(x = (1-i)y\).  
6. Choose \(y = 1\), so eigenvector is \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1-i \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\); the complex conjugate eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2-i\) is \(\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1+i \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).  
7. Form the matrix \(P\) with columns as the real and imaginary parts of \(\vec{v}_1\): \(\operatorname{Re}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\operatorname{Im}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\), so \(P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).  
8. Verify \(P\) is invertible: \(\det P = (1)(0) - (1)(-1) = 1\), so \(P\) is invertible.  
9. The matrix \(C\) is \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), matching the form with \(a = 2\), \(b = 1\).  
10. Verify \(A = P C P^{-1}\): \(P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\), so \(PC = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\), then \(PCP^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = A\), confirming correctness.  
11. Final answer: An invertible matrix \(P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) satisfy \(A = P C P^{-1}\).
