To construct a \(4 \times 3\) matrix of rank 1, recall that a matrix has rank 1 if and only if all its rows (or columns) are scalar multiples of a single nonzero row (or column); let's choose a nonzero row vector \(\mathbf{v} = [1\ 2\ 3]\) and a nonzero column vector \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\), then the matrix \(A = \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}\) will have rank 1.

Calculate the outer product: \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} [1\ 2\ 3] = \begin{bmatrix} 2\times1 & 2\times2 & 2\times3 \\ -1\times1 & -1\times2 & -1\times3 \\ 0\times1 & 0\times2 & 0\times3 \\ 4\times1 & 4\times2 & 4\times3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ -1 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 4 & 8 & 12 \end{bmatrix}\). 
3. Verify the rank: All rows are scalar multiples of the first row (\([2\ 4\ 6]\)), e.g., second row is \(-0.5\) times the first, third row is zero, fourth row is 2 times the first, so the row space is one-dimensional, confirming rank 1. 
4. Final answer: A \(4 \times 3\) matrix with rank 1 is \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ -1 & -2 & -3 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 4 & 8 & 12 \end{bmatrix}\).
