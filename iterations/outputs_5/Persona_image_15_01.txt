1. First, find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\).
2. Solve the quadratic equation \(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\) to get the eigenvalues: \(\lambda = 2 \pm i\).
3. Since \(C\) must be of the form \(\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix}\), set \(a = 2\), \(b = 1\) so that its eigenvalues are \(2 \pm i\).
4. Find an eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2 + i\) by solving \((A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0\): \(A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}\).
5. Set up the equation \(\begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\), which gives \((-1-i)x - 2y = 0\) and \(x + (1-i)y = 0\).
6. Solve for \(x\) in terms of \(y\) using the second equation: \(x = -(1-i)y\).
7. Substitute \(x\) into the first equation: \((-1-i)(-(1-i)y) - 2y = (1+i)(1-i)y - 2y = (1 - i^2)y - 2y = (1+1)y - 2y = 2y - 2y = 0\), so any \(y \neq 0\) works.
8. Choose \(y = 1\), so \(x = -(1-i)\), giving the eigenvector \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1-i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).
9. The complex conjugate eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2 - i\) is \(\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1+i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\).
10. Form the matrix \(P\) using the real and imaginary parts of \(\vec{v}_1\): \(\operatorname{Re}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\operatorname{Im}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\), so \(P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\).
11. The matrix \(C\) is \(\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\) since it has eigenvalues \(2 \pm i\).
12. The answer is: \(P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\), \(C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\), and \(A = P C P^{-1}\).
