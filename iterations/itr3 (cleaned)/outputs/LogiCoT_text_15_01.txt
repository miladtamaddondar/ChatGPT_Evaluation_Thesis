1. Find the eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\).  
2. Solve the quadratic equation \(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0\): The solutions are \(\lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16-20}}{2} = 2 \pm i\).  
3. Since the eigenvalues are complex conjugates, \(A\) is similar to a real matrix of the form \(C = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix}\) with \(a = \operatorname{Re}(\lambda) = 2\), \(b = \operatorname{Im}(\lambda) = 1\), so \(C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).  
4. Find an eigenvector for \(\lambda = 2 + i\): Solve \((A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0\), so \(A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}\).  
5. Set up the system: \((-1-i)x - 2y = 0\) and \(x + (1-i)y = 0\); from the second, \(x = -(1-i)y\), substitute into the first: \((-1-i)(-(1-i)y) - 2y = 0\).  
6. Expand: \((1+i)(1-i)y - 2y = (1^2 - i^2)y - 2y = (1 - (-1))y - 2y = (2 - 2)y = 0\), so any nonzero \(y\) works; choose \(y = 1\), then \(x = -(1-i)\).  
7. The eigenvector is \(\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} -(1-i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\); its real and imaginary parts are \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}\), \(\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}\).  
8. Form the invertible matrix \(P = [\vec{v}_1 \ \vec{v}_2] = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and verify \(\det P = (-1)(0) - (1)(1) = -1 \neq 0\), so \(P\) is invertible.  
9. Verify that \(P^{-1}AP = C\): Compute \(P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-1}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\), then \(C = P^{-1}AP = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).  
10. Final answer: \(A = P C P^{-1}\) with \(P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\).
