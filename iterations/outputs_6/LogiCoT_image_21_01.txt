1. We are given \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -2 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 \\ 3 & -2 & -5 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 7 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\), and need to solve \(A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}\) for \(\mathbf{x}\).  
2. To determine if the solution is unique, we check if \(A\) is invertible by computing \(\det(A)\).  
3. Compute \(\det(A)\): \(1 \cdot (1 \cdot -5 - 6 \cdot -2) - 0 \cdot (-2 \cdot -5 - 6 \cdot 3) + (-2) \cdot (-2 \cdot -2 - 1 \cdot 3) = 1(-5 + 12) - 0 + (-2)(4 - 3) = 1(7) + (-2)(1) = 7 - 2 = 5\).  
4. Since \(\det(A) = 5 \neq 0\), \(A\) is invertible, so the solution \(\mathbf{x}\) is unique by the Invertible Matrix Theorem.  
5. The solution is \(\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}\); we now compute \(A^{-1}\).  
6. The inverse of \(A\) is \(\frac{1}{\det(A)} \operatorname{adj}(A)\), so we compute the adjugate matrix \(\operatorname{adj}(A)\).  
7. Compute the matrix of cofactors for \(A\):     - \(C_{11} = \det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 6 \\ -2 & -5 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(-5) - (6)(-2) = -5 + 12 = 7\)     - \(C_{12} = -\det\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 6 \\ 3 & -5 \end{bmatrix} = -((-2)(-5) - (6)(3)) = -(10 - 18) = 8\)     - \(C_{13} = \det\begin{bmatrix} -2 & 1 \\ 3 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = (-2)(-2) - (1)(3) = 4 - 3 = 1\)     - \(C_{21} = -\det\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -2 \\ -2 & -5 \end{bmatrix} = -((0)(-5) - (-2)(-2)) = -(0 - 4) = 4\)     - \(C_{22} = \det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 3 & -5 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(-5) - (-2)(3) = -5 + 6 = 1\)     - \(C_{23} = -\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 3 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = -((1)(-2) - (0)(3)) = -(-2 - 0) = 2\)     - \(C_{31} = \det\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -2 \\ 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = (0)(6) - (1)(-2) = 0 + 2 = 2\)     - \(C_{32} = -\det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = -((1)(6) - (-2)(-2)) = -(6 - 4) = -2\)     - \(C_{33} = \det\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = (1)(1) - (0)(-2) = 1 - 0 = 1\)  
8. The cofactor matrix is \(\begin{bmatrix} 7 & 8 & 1 \\ 4 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\), so the adjugate is its transpose: \(\operatorname{adj}(A) = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 4 & 2 \\ 8 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).  
9. Thus, \(A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 4 & 2 \\ 8 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\).  
10. Multiply \(A^{-1}\) by \(\mathbf{b}\): \(\mathbf{x} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 4 & 2 \\ 8 & 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 7 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\).  
11. Compute each entry:      - First entry: \(7(-1) + 4(7) + 2(-3) = -7 + 28 - 6 = 15\)      - Second entry: \(8(-1) + 1(7) + (-2)(-3) = -8 + 7 + 6 = 5\)      - Third entry: \(1(-1) + 2(7) + 1(-3) = -1 + 14 - 3 = 10\)  
12. So, \(\mathbf{x} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 15 \\ 5 \\ 10 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\).  
13. The unique solution is \(\boxed{\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}}\), and the solution is unique because \(A\) is invertible.
