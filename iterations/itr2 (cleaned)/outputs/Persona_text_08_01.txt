1. To construct a \( 4 \times 3 \) matrix with rank 1, recall that a matrix has rank 1 if all its rows (or columns) are scalar multiples of a single nonzero row (or column), so let's choose a nonzero column vector \( \mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \) and a nonzero row vector \( \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \). 
2. The outer product \( \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T \) gives a \( 4 \times 3 \) matrix where each entry is \( u_i v_j \), so \( A = \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \times 5 & 1 \times 6 & 1 \times 7 \\ 2 \times 5 & 2 \times 6 & 2 \times 7 \\ 3 \times 5 & 3 \times 6 & 3 \times 7 \\ 4 \times 5 & 4 \times 6 & 4 \times 7 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 10 & 12 & 14 \\ 15 & 18 & 21 \\ 20 & 24 & 28 \end{bmatrix} \). 
3. Since every row of \( A \) is a scalar multiple of the first row, the row space is one-dimensional, so the rank of \( A \) is 1. 
4. Therefore, a \( 4 \times 3 \) matrix with rank 1 is \( \boxed{\begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 & 7 \\ 10 & 12 & 14 \\ 15 & 18 & 21 \\ 20 & 24 & 28 \end{bmatrix}} \).
