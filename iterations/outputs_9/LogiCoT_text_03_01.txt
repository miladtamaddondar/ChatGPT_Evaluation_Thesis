1. Let \( A = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 9 \\ 4 & 5\end{bmatrix} \); we seek matrices \( L \) (lower triangular with 1s on the diagonal) and \( U \) (upper triangular) such that \( A = LU \).
2. Assume \( L = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ l_{21} & 1\end{bmatrix} \) and \( U = \begin{bmatrix}u_{11} & u_{12} \\ 0 & u_{22}\end{bmatrix} \); then \( LU = \begin{bmatrix}u_{11} & u_{12} \\ l_{21}u_{11} & l_{21}u_{12} + u_{22}\end{bmatrix} \).
3. Set \( u_{11} = 6 \) and \( u_{12} = 9 \) by matching the first row of \( LU \) to \( A \); this is valid since the first row of \( L \) is \([1, 0]\).
4. Solve for \( l_{21} \) using \( l_{21}u_{11} = 4 \implies l_{21} = \frac{4}{6} = \frac{2}{3} \); this matches the (2,1) entry of \( A \).
5. Solve for \( u_{22} \) using \( l_{21}u_{12} + u_{22} = 5 \implies \frac{2}{3} \times 9 + u_{22} = 5 \implies 6 + u_{22} = 5 \implies u_{22} = -1 \); this matches the (2,2) entry of \( A \).
6. Thus, \( L = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ \frac{2}{3} & 1\end{bmatrix} \) and \( U = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 9 \\ 0 & -1\end{bmatrix} \); verify \( LU = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 9 \\ 4 & 5\end{bmatrix} = A \), confirming correctness.
7. Final answer: The LU factorization is \( \boxed{L = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ \frac{2}{3} & 1\end{bmatrix},\ U = \begin{bmatrix}6 & 9 \\ 0 & -1\end{bmatrix}} \).
