To find a basis of \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\), first find the eigenvalues of \(A\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\left(\begin{bmatrix}0.6-\lambda & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7-\lambda\end{bmatrix}\right) = (0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0\).
2. Expanding the determinant gives \((0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = \lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\).
3. Solve the quadratic equation \(\lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\) to get \(\lambda = 1\) and \(\lambda = 0.3\).
4. Since \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}\) is already given as an eigenvector, and since \(A\) is stochastic, \(\mathbf{v}_1\) corresponds to \(\lambda = 1\).
5. To find another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) for \(\lambda = 0.3\), solve \((A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0\): \(A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\).
6. Set up the equation \(\begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}\), which gives \(0.3x + 0.3y = 0\) and \(0.4x + 0.4y = 0\).
7. Both equations reduce to \(x + y = 0\), so \(y = -x\), and thus an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\).
8. Therefore, a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\) is \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\right\}\).
