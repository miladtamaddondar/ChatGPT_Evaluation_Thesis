1. To find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB = 0\), each column of \(B\) must be in the null space of \(A\), since \(A\) times any column of \(B\) must yield the zero vector.
2. Let the first column of \(B\) be \(\mathbf{b}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}\); then \(A\mathbf{b}_1 = 0\) gives the system: \(3x_1 - 6x_2 = 0\) and \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\).
3. Solving \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\) gives \(x_1 = 2x_2\); substituting into the first equation: \(3(2x_2) - 6x_2 = 0 \implies 6x_2 - 6x_2 = 0\), which is always true, so the null space is spanned by \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).
4. To use two different nonzero columns, pick two distinct nonzero scalar multiples: let \(\mathbf{b}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\).
5. Construct \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\), where both columns are nonzero and linearly dependent but distinct.
6. Verify: \(A\mathbf{b}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3(2) + (-6)(1) \\ -1(2) + 2(1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 - 6 \\ -2 + 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
7. Similarly, \(A\mathbf{b}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3(4) + (-6)(2) \\ -1(4) + 2(2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 - 12 \\ -4 + 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
8. Thus, \(AB = 0\) with \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\), and both columns are nonzero and different, as required.
