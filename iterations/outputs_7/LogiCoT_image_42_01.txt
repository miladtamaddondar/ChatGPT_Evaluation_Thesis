1. To write \(\mathbf{y}\) as the sum of a vector in \(W\) and a vector orthogonal to \(W\), we need to project \(\mathbf{y}\) onto the subspace \(W\) spanned by \(\mathbf{u}_1, \mathbf{u}_2, \mathbf{u}_3\), and subtract this projection from \(\mathbf{y}\); this uses the fundamental theorem of orthogonal projections in linear algebra.
2. Let \(U = [\mathbf{u}_1\ \mathbf{u}_2\ \mathbf{u}_3]\) be the \(4 \times 3\) matrix whose columns are the basis vectors of \(W\); the projection of \(\mathbf{y}\) onto \(W\) is given by \(\mathbf{y}_W = U(U^T U)^{-1}U^T \mathbf{y}\).
3. Compute \(U^T U\): \(U^T U = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{u}_1^T \\ \mathbf{u}_2^T \\ \mathbf{u}_3^T \end{bmatrix} [\mathbf{u}_1\ \mathbf{u}_2\ \mathbf{u}_3]\), which gives a \(3 \times 3\) matrix whose \((i,j)\) entry is \(\mathbf{u}_i^T \mathbf{u}_j\).
4. Calculate each entry: \(\mathbf{u}_1^T \mathbf{u}_1 = 1^2 + 1^2 + 0^2 + 1^2 = 3\), \(\mathbf{u}_1^T \mathbf{u}_2 = 1 \cdot (-1) + 1 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-2) = -1 + 3 + 0 - 2 = 0\), \(\mathbf{u}_1^T \mathbf{u}_3 = 1 \cdot (-1) + 1 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 = -1 + 0 + 0 + 1 = 0\).
5. Similarly, \(\mathbf{u}_2^T \mathbf{u}_2 = (-1)^2 + 3^2 + 1^2 + (-2)^2 = 1 + 9 + 1 + 4 = 15\), \(\mathbf{u}_2^T \mathbf{u}_3 = (-1) \cdot (-1) + 3 \cdot 0 + 1 \cdot 1 + (-2) \cdot 1 = 1 + 0 + 1 - 2 = 0\), \(\mathbf{u}_3^T \mathbf{u}_3 = (-1)^2 + 0^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 = 1 + 0 + 1 + 1 = 3\).
6. Therefore, \(U^T U = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 15 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\), which is diagonal, confirming the \(\mathbf{u}_i\) are orthogonal.
7. The inverse is \( (U^T U)^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/15 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix} \), since the inverse of a diagonal matrix is the reciprocal of the diagonal entries.
8. Compute \(U^T \mathbf{y}\): \(\mathbf{u}_1^T \mathbf{y} = 1 \cdot 4 + 1 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot (-1) = 4 + 3 + 0 - 1 = 6\), \(\mathbf{u}_2^T \mathbf{y} = (-1) \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot 3 + (-2) \cdot (-1) = -4 + 9 + 3 + 2 = 10\), \(\mathbf{u}_3^T \mathbf{y} = (-1) \cdot 4 + 0 \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot (-1) = -4 + 0 + 3 - 1 = -2\).
9. Therefore, \(U^T \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 10 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}\).
10. Multiply \( (U^T U)^{-1} U^T \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/3 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/15 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1/3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 10 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 2/3 \\ -2/3 \end{bmatrix} \).
11. The projection coefficients are \(c_1 = 2\), \(c_2 = 2/3\), \(c_3 = -2/3\), so the projection is \(\mathbf{y}_W = 2\mathbf{u}_1 + \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{u}_2 - \frac{2}{3}\mathbf{u}_3\).
12. Compute \(\mathbf{y}_W = 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{2}{3}\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{2}{3}\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -2/3 \\ 2 \\ 2/3 \\ -4/3 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2/3 \\ 0 \\ -2/3 \\ -2/3 \end{bmatrix}\).
13. Add the vectors: first entry: \(2 - 2/3 + 2/3 = 2\); second: \(2 + 2 + 0 = 4\); third: \(0 + 2/3 - 2/3 = 0\); fourth: \(2 - 4/3 - 2/3 = 2 - 2 = 0\).
14. So, \(\mathbf{y}_W = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
15. The orthogonal component is \(\mathbf{y}_{W^\perp} = \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{y}_W = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\).
16. Final answer: \(\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\), where the first vector is in \(W\) and the second is orthogonal to \(W\).
