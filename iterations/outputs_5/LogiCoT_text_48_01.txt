1. To find a nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$ orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}_1$ and $\mathbf{u}_2$, we solve $\mathbf{u}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$ and $\mathbf{u}_2 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$ for $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix}$.
2. Write the orthogonality equations: $\mathbf{u}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 1x + 1y + (-2)z = 0$ and $\mathbf{u}_2 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 5x + (-1)y + 2z = 0$.
3. The system of equations is: $x + y - 2z = 0$ and $5x - y + 2z = 0$.
4. Add the two equations: $(x + y - 2z) + (5x - y + 2z) = 0 + 0 \implies 6x = 0 \implies x = 0$.
5. Substitute $x = 0$ into the first equation: $0 + y - 2z = 0 \implies y = 2z$.
6. Substitute $x = 0$ and $y = 2z$ into the second equation: $5(0) - (2z) + 2z = 0 \implies 0 = 0$, which is always true, confirming consistency.
7. Thus, the general solution is $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2z \\ z \end{bmatrix} = z \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ for any $z \neq 0$.
8. Choosing $z = 1$, a nonzero vector is $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, which is orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}_1$ and $\mathbf{u}_2$.
