1. To find a basis \(\mathcal{B}\) such that \([T]_{\mathcal{B}}\) is diagonal, we must find the eigenvectors of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\).
2. Compute the characteristic polynomial: \(\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{bmatrix} -\lambda & 1 \\ -3 & 4-\lambda \end{bmatrix} = (-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3\).
3. Set the characteristic polynomial to zero and solve: \(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0\).
4. Factor the quadratic: \((\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0\), so the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = 3\).
5. Find the eigenvector for \(\lambda_1 = 1\): solve \((A - I)\vec{v} = 0\), i.e., \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\vec{v} = 0\).
6. The first row gives \(-v_1 + v_2 = 0\), so \(v_2 = v_1\); thus, an eigenvector is \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).
7. Find the eigenvector for \(\lambda_2 = 3\): solve \((A - 3I)\vec{v} = 0\), i.e., \(\begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\vec{v} = 0\).
8. The first row gives \(-3v_1 + v_2 = 0\), so \(v_2 = 3v_1\); thus, an eigenvector is \(\vec{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\).
9. Therefore, the basis \(\mathcal{B} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) diagonalizes \(A\), so \([T]_{\mathcal{B}}\) is diagonal.
