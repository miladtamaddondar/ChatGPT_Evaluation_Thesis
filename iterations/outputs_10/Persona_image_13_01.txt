1. To find a basis for the eigenspace for \(\lambda = 4\), solve \((A - 4I)\vec{x} = 0\).
2. Compute \(A - 4I = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
3. Set up the system \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
4. The fourth variable \(x_4\) is free since the last row is all zeros.
5. The third equation gives \(x_2 - 3x_3 = 0 \implies x_2 = 3x_3\).
6. The second equation gives \(x_1 - x_2 + x_3 = 0 \implies x_1 = x_2 - x_3 = 3x_3 - x_3 = 2x_3\).
7. The first equation gives \(-x_1 + 2x_3 = 0 \implies x_1 = 2x_3\), which is consistent with the previous step.
8. Let \(x_3 = t\) and \(x_4 = s\), so the general solution is \(\begin{bmatrix} 2t \\ 3t \\ t \\ s \end{bmatrix} = t\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + s\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).
9. Therefore, a basis for the eigenspace corresponding to \(\lambda = 4\) is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
