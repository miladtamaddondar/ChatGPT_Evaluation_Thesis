1. To find a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$ consisting of $\mathbf{v}_1$ and another eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_2$ of $A$, we first need to find all eigenvalues of $A$ by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$.
2. Compute $A - \lambda I = \begin{bmatrix}0.6 - \lambda & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7 - \lambda\end{bmatrix}$.
3. Compute the determinant: $(0.6 - \lambda)(0.7 - \lambda) - (0.4)(0.3) = 0$.
4. Expand: $(0.6 - \lambda)(0.7 - \lambda) = 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 = 0.42 - 1.3\lambda + \lambda^2$.
5. Subtract $0.12$ (since $0.4 \times 0.3 = 0.12$): $0.42 - 1.3\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = 0$.
6. Simplify: $0.3 - 1.3\lambda + \lambda^2 = 0$.
7. Rearranged: $\lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0$.
8. Solve the quadratic equation: $\lambda = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{(1.3)^2 - 4 \times 0.3}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.69 - 1.2}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{0.49}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm 0.7}{2}$.
9. Thus, the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 0.3$.
10. We are given $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}$, which is an eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 1$.
11. To find an eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_2$ for $\lambda_2 = 0.3$, solve $(A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0$.
12. Compute $A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}$.
13. Write the system: $0.3x + 0.3y = 0$ and $0.4x + 0.4y = 0$.
14. Both equations are equivalent, so $x + y = 0$.
15. Thus, an eigenvector is any nonzero scalar multiple of $\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}$.
16. To make the basis explicit, let $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}$.
17. Therefore, a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$ consisting of $\mathbf{v}_1$ and another eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_2$ of $A$ is $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \right\}$.
