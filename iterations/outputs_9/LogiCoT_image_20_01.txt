1. To find a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\), first find all eigenvalues of \(A\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\begin{bmatrix}0.6-\lambda & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - (0.3)(0.4) = 0\).
2. Expanding the determinant: \((0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = \lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\).
3. Solve the quadratic equation \(\lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\) using the quadratic formula: \(\lambda = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{(1.3)^2 - 4 \cdot 0.3}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.69 - 1.2}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{0.49}}{2}\).
4. Since \(\sqrt{0.49} = 0.7\), the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = \frac{1.3 + 0.7}{2} = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = \frac{1.3 - 0.7}{2} = 0.3\).
5. We are given that \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}\) is an eigenvector, and since \(A\) is stochastic, \(\mathbf{v}_1\) corresponds to \(\lambda_1 = 1\); verify: \(A\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}0.6 \cdot \frac{3}{7} + 0.3 \cdot \frac{4}{7} \\ 0.4 \cdot \frac{3}{7} + 0.7 \cdot \frac{4}{7}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{1.8+1.2}{7} \\ \frac{1.2+2.8}{7}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}\frac{3}{7} \\ \frac{4}{7}\end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{v}_1\).
6. To find another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) for \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\), solve \((A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0\): \(A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\).
7. Set up the system: \(\begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}\), which gives \(0.3x + 0.3y = 0\) and \(0.4x + 0.4y = 0\).
8. Both equations reduce to \(x + y = 0\), so a nonzero solution is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\).
9. Verify that \(\mathbf{v}_2\) is an eigenvector: \(A\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0.6 \cdot 1 + 0.3 \cdot (-1) \\ 0.4 \cdot 1 + 0.7 \cdot (-1)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.6 - 0.3 \\ 0.4 - 0.7\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 \\ -0.3\end{bmatrix} = 0.3\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\).
10. Therefore, a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\) is \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\right\}\).
