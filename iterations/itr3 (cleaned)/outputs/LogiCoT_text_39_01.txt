1. Recognize that $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T$ is the outer product of a $2 \times 1$ vector and a $3 \times 1$ vector, resulting in a $2 \times 3$ matrix: $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 v_1 & u_1 v_2 & u_1 v_3 \\ u_2 v_1 & u_2 v_2 & u_2 v_3 \end{bmatrix}$.
2. Set $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}$, so $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot v_1 & 1 \cdot v_2 & 1 \cdot v_3 \\ 2 \cdot v_1 & 2 \cdot v_2 & 2 \cdot v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix}$.
3. Equate this to the given matrix: $\begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$.
4. Set up equations by matching corresponding entries: $v_1 = 1$, $v_2 = -3$, $v_3 = 4$, $2v_1 = 2$, $2v_2 = -6$, $2v_3 = 8$.
5. Verify that $v_1 = 1$ gives $2v_1 = 2$, $v_2 = -3$ gives $2v_2 = -6$, and $v_3 = 4$ gives $2v_3 = 8$, confirming consistency.
6. Conclude that the solution is $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$.
