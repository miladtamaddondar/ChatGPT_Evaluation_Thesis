1. To find another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\), first compute the eigenvalues by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\left(\begin{bmatrix}0.6-\lambda & 0.3\\0.4 & 0.7-\lambda\end{bmatrix}\right) = (0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - (0.3)(0.4) = 0\).
2. Expand and simplify: \((0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0 \implies 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = 0 \implies \lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\).
3. Solve the quadratic: \(\lambda = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.3^2 - 4 \cdot 0.3}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.69 - 1.2}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm 0.7}{2}\), so eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1\), \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\).
4. For \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\), solve \((A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0\): \(A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3\\0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\).
5. Write the system: \(0.3x + 0.3y = 0\), \(0.4x + 0.4y = 0\), which reduces to \(x + y = 0\).
6. Thus, an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix}\).
7. Therefore, a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of eigenvectors is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}\frac{3}{7}\\\frac{4}{7}\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\-1\end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
