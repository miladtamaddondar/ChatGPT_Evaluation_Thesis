1. To find $\mathbf{x}$ such that $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, we set up the equation: $\begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 & -2 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 \\ 3 & -2 & -5\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-1 \\ 7 \\ -3\end{bmatrix}$, which is a linear system.  
2. Write the augmented matrix: $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 & 7 \\ 3 & -2 & -5 & -3 \end{array}\right]$; this is correct as it represents the system.  
3. Use row 1 to eliminate $x_1$ from rows 2 and 3: Row 2 $\to$ Row 2 + 2(Row 1): $(-2)+2(1)=0$, $1+2(0)=1$, $6+2(-2)=2$, $7+2(-1)=5$; Row 3 $\to$ Row 3 - 3(Row 1): $3-3(1)=0$, $-2-3(0)=-2$, $-5-3(-2)=1$, $-3-3(-1)=0$.  
4. The new matrix is $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & -2 & 1 & 0 \end{array}\right]$; this is verified by direct calculation.  
5. Use row 2 to eliminate $x_2$ from row 3: Row 3 $\to$ Row 3 + 2(Row 2): $-2+2(1)=0$, $1+2(2)=5$, $0+2(5)=10$; so Row 3 is $[0, 0, 5, 10]$.  
6. The matrix is now $\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 5 & 10 \end{array}\right]$; this is correct by row operations.  
7. Solve for $x_3$ from row 3: $5x_3 = 10 \implies x_3 = 2$; this follows from basic algebra.  
8. Substitute $x_3 = 2$ into row 2: $x_2 + 2x_3 = 5 \implies x_2 + 4 = 5 \implies x_2 = 1$; verified by substitution.  
9. Substitute $x_3 = 2$ into row 1: $x_1 - 2x_3 = -1 \implies x_1 - 4 = -1 \implies x_1 = 3$; verified by substitution.  
10. The solution is $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}3 \\ 1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}$; this is found by back-substitution.  
11. To determine uniqueness, check if $A$ is invertible: the matrix reduced to row-echelon form with all pivots nonzero, so $\operatorname{rank}(A) = 3$; since $A$ is $3 \times 3$ and full rank, the solution is unique.  
12. Final answer: The unique solution is $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}3 \\ 1 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}$.
