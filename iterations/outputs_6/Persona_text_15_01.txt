1. First, find the eigenvalues of $A$ by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $\det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$.
2. Solve for $\lambda$: $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \implies \lambda = 2 \pm i$.
3. Since the eigenvalues are complex conjugates, $C$ must be $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
4. Find an eigenvector for $\lambda = 2 + i$ by solving $(A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0$: $A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}$.
5. Set $\vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, then $(-1-i)x - 2y = 0$ and $x + (1-i)y = 0$.
6. From the first equation, $(-1-i)x = 2y \implies x = \frac{-2y}{1+i}$.
7. Rationalize denominator: $x = \frac{-2y}{1+i} \cdot \frac{1-i}{1-i} = \frac{-2y(1-i)}{(1+i)(1-i)} = \frac{-2y(1-i)}{2} = -y(1-i)$.
8. Choose $y=1$, then $x = -(1-i)$, so an eigenvector is $\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1-i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
9. The complex conjugate eigenvector for $\lambda = 2 - i$ is $\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1+i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
10. Form $P$ using the real and imaginary parts of $\vec{v}_1$: $\operatorname{Re}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\operatorname{Im}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$, so $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
11. The matrix $C$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
12. The answer: $A = P C P^{-1}$ with $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
