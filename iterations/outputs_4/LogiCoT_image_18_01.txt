1. To diagonalize \( A \), we first find its eigenvalues by solving \( \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \): \(\det\begin{bmatrix} 0-\lambda & 1 \\ -3 & 4-\lambda \end{bmatrix} = (0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0\).  
2. Factoring the quadratic, we get \((\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0\), so the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = 3\); this is correct since the characteristic polynomial factors cleanly.  
3. For \(\lambda_1 = 1\), solve \((A - I)\vec{v} = 0\): \(A - I = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ -3 & 3 \end{bmatrix}\), so \(-v_1 + v_2 = 0\) and \(-3v_1 + 3v_2 = 0\); both equations are multiples, so any vector with \(v_2 = v_1\) is an eigenvector.  
4. Choose \(v_1 = 1\), so an eigenvector for \(\lambda_1 = 1\) is \(\vec{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\); verify: \(A\vec{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 1\vec{v}_1\).  
5. For \(\lambda_2 = 3\), solve \((A - 3I)\vec{v} = 0\): \(A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1 \\ -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\), so \(-3v_1 + v_2 = 0\); any vector with \(v_2 = 3v_1\) is an eigenvector.  
6. Choose \(v_1 = 1\), so an eigenvector for \(\lambda_2 = 3\) is \(\vec{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\); verify: \(A\vec{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} = 3\vec{v}_2\).  
7. The basis \(\mathcal{B} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) diagonalizes \(A\), since these are linearly independent eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues.  
8. Final answer: A basis \(\mathcal{B}\) for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) such that \([T]_{\mathcal{B}}\) is diagonal is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
