1. The standard matrix for a counterclockwise rotation by angle $\theta$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is $\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$; verify by applying this to the standard basis vectors and confirming the rotated coordinates.  
2. Substitute $\theta = \frac{3\pi}{2}$ to get $\cos\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = 0$ and $\sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = -1$; verify using the unit circle that at $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ radians, the point is $(0, -1)$.  
3. Substitute these values into the rotation matrix to obtain $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -(-1) \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$; verify by checking that this matrix rotates $(1,0)$ to $(0,-1)$ and $(0,1)$ to $(1,0)$, which matches a $270^\circ$ (or $\frac{3\pi}{2}$) counterclockwise rotation.  
4. The standard matrix of $T$ is $\boxed{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}$; verify that the determinant is $0^2 - (1)(-1) = 1$, confirming the matrix is invertible and thus a valid rotation matrix.
