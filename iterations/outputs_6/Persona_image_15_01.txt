1. First, find the eigenvalues of \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \) by solving \( \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \): \( \det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \).
2. Solve the quadratic equation \( \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \) to get \( \lambda = 2 \pm i \).
3. Since the eigenvalues are complex conjugates, \( C \) must have the form \( \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix} \) with \( a = 2 \), \( b = 1 \), so \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \).
4. Find an eigenvector for \( \lambda = 2+i \) by solving \( (A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0 \): \( \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}\vec{v} = 0 \).
5. Let \( v_1 = 1 \), then \( -1-i - 2v_2 = 0 \implies v_2 = \frac{-1-i}{2} \), so the eigenvector is \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ \frac{-1-i}{2} \end{pmatrix} \).
6. The real and imaginary parts of this eigenvector are \( \vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \) and \( \vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \), so set \( P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \).
7. The matrices \( P \) and \( C \) satisfy \( A = P C P^{-1} \) with \( P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \) and \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \).
