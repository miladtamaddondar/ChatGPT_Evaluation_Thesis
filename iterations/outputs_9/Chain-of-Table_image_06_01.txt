1. To find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB = 0\), we need two different nonzero vectors in the null space of \(A\). Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\). Solve \(A\mathbf{x} = 0\): \(\begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).
2. Write the augmented matrix: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 3 & -6 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 0 \end{array}\right]\).
3. Row 2 = Row 2 + \frac{1}{3} Row 1: \(\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 3 & -6 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]\).
4. The first row gives \(3x_1 - 6x_2 = 0\), so \(x_1 = 2x_2\). The general solution is \(\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).
5. Choose two different nonzero values for \(x_2\), say \(x_2 = 1\) and \(x_2 = -1\), to get two different columns: \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\).
6. Thus, a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) is \(\boxed{B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}}\), and \(AB = 0\) with two different nonzero columns.
