1. To find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB = 0\), each column of \(B\) must be in the null space of \(A\), so we solve \(A\mathbf{x} = 0\).  
2. Let \(\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}\), then \(A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3x_1 - 6x_2 \\ -x_1 + 2x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).  
3. Set up the system: \(3x_1 - 6x_2 = 0\) and \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\).  
4. Solve the second equation for \(x_1\): \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0 \implies x_1 = 2x_2\).  
5. Substitute \(x_1 = 2x_2\) into the first equation: \(3(2x_2) - 6x_2 = 6x_2 - 6x_2 = 0\), so any vector of the form \(\begin{bmatrix} 2x_2 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}\) is a solution.  
6. Thus, the null space of \(A\) is spanned by \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\), so we need two different nonzero columns for \(B\), both multiples of \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\).  
7. Choose two different nonzero scalars, say \(a = 1\) and \(b = -3\), so the columns are \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} -6 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix}\).  
8. Therefore, one possible matrix \(B\) is \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -6 \\ 1 & -3 \end{bmatrix}\).
