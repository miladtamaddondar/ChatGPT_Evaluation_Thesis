1. To find a basis for the eigenspace for \(\lambda = 4\), solve \((A - 4I)\vec{x} = 0\).  
2. Compute \(A - 4I = \begin{bmatrix} 3-4 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & 3-4 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1-4 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 4-4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 2 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\).  
3. Set up the system: \(-x_1 + 2x_3 = 0\), \(x_1 - x_2 + x_3 = 0\), \(x_2 - 3x_3 = 0\), \(0 = 0\).  
4. Solve \(x_2 - 3x_3 = 0 \implies x_2 = 3x_3\).  
5. Substitute \(x_2 = 3x_3\) into \(x_1 - x_2 + x_3 = 0\) to get \(x_1 - 3x_3 + x_3 = 0 \implies x_1 = 2x_3\).  
6. Substitute \(x_1 = 2x_3\) into \(-x_1 + 2x_3 = 0\) to check: \(-2x_3 + 2x_3 = 0\), which is satisfied for all \(x_3\).  
7. The general solution is \(\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_3 \\ 3x_3 \\ x_3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\).  
8. Therefore, a basis for the eigenspace corresponding to \(\lambda = 4\) is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) since any vector with \(x_4\) free and the rest zero is also a solution.
