2. Write the orthogonality equations: $\mathbf{u}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 1x + 1y + (-2)z = 0$ and $\mathbf{u}_2 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 5x + (-1)y + 2z = 0$.
3. This gives the system: $x + y - 2z = 0$ and $5x - y + 2z = 0$.
4. Add the two equations to eliminate $y$: $(x + y - 2z) + (5x - y + 2z) = 0 \implies 6x = 0 \implies x = 0$.
5. Substitute $x = 0$ into the first equation: $0 + y - 2z = 0 \implies y = 2z$.
6. Thus, any vector of the form $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2z \\ z \end{bmatrix}$ is orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}_1$ and $\mathbf{u}_2$.
7. Choose $z = 1$ (to get a nonzero vector): $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$.
8. Verify: $\mathbf{u}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 1 \cdot 0 + 1 \cdot 2 + (-2) \cdot 1 = 0$ and $\mathbf{u}_2 \cdot \mathbf{v} = 5 \cdot 0 + (-1) \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 1 = 0$, confirming orthogonality.
9. Final answer: A nonzero vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}_1$ and $\mathbf{u}_2$ is $\boxed{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}$.
