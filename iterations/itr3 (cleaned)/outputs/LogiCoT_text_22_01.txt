1. Recall that the standard matrix for a counterclockwise rotation by angle $\theta$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is $\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$; this follows from how rotation transforms the standard basis vectors.  
2. Substitute $\theta = \frac{3\pi}{2}$ into the matrix to get $\begin{bmatrix} \cos\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) & -\sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) \\ \sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) & \cos\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) \end{bmatrix}$; this step uses the definition of the rotation matrix.  
3. Compute $\cos\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = 0$ and $\sin\left(\frac{3\pi}{2}\right) = -1$; this follows from the unit circle values at $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ radians.  
4. Substitute these values to get the matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -(-1) \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$; this uses arithmetic and properties of sine and cosine.  
5. Verify correctness: the determinant is $0 \cdot 0 - (1)(-1) = 1$, which matches the property that rotation matrices have determinant 1; the rank is 2, so the transformation is invertible; the eigenvalues are $\pm i$, which is consistent with a $270^\circ$ rotation.  
6. The standard matrix of $T$ is $\boxed{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}$.
