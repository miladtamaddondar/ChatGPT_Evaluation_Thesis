1. To diagonalize $A$, first find its eigenvalues by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $\det\begin{bmatrix}0-\lambda & 1 \\ -3 & 4-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$.
2. Factor the characteristic polynomial: $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, so the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$.
3. For $\lambda_1 = 1$, solve $(A - I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - I = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & 1 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix}$, so $(-1)v_1 + v_2 = 0$ and $-3v_1 + 3v_2 = 0$.
4. Both equations are multiples, so $v_2 = v_1$; thus, an eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 1$ is $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$.
5. For $\lambda_2 = 3$, solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix}-3 & 1 \\ -3 & 1\end{bmatrix}$, so $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$.
6. Thus, $v_2 = 3v_1$; an eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 3$ is $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}$.
7. The set $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$ forms a basis of eigenvectors since they are linearly independent (their determinant is $1 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot 1 = 2 \neq 0$).
8. In this basis, $[T]_{\mathcal{B}}$ is diagonal with the eigenvalues on the diagonal: $[T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3\end{bmatrix}$.
9. Final answer: A basis $\mathcal{B}$ for $\mathbb{R}^2$ so that $[T]_{\mathcal{B}}$ is diagonal is $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$.
