1. To find $P$ and $C$, first compute the eigenvalues of $A$ by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $\det\left(\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix}\right) = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$.
2. The characteristic equation $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$ has roots $\lambda = 2 \pm i$; these are complex conjugate eigenvalues, which matches the form of $C$.
3. The matrix $C$ should be $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, since $a = 2$, $b = 1$ correspond to eigenvalues $2 \pm i$.
4. To find $P$, compute an eigenvector for $\lambda = 2 + i$: solve $(A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0$, so $A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}$.
5. Set up the system: $(-1-i)x - 2y = 0$ and $x + (1-i)y = 0$; from the second equation, $x = -(1-i)y$.
6. Substitute $x$ into the first equation: $(-1-i)(-(1-i)y) - 2y = (1-i+1+i)y - 2y = (2 - 2y) = 0$, so $y$ is arbitrary (as expected for eigenvectors).
7. Choose $y = 1$, then $x = -(1-i)$, so an eigenvector is $\vec{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1-i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
8. The complex conjugate eigenvector for $2-i$ is $\vec{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -(1+i) \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
9. Form $P$ using the real and imaginary parts of $\vec{v}_1$: $\operatorname{Re}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$, $\operatorname{Im}(\vec{v}_1) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$, so $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
10. Verify $P$ is invertible: $\det(P) = (-1)(0) - (1)(1) = -1 \neq 0$.
11. The real canonical form is $C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
12. Therefore, $A = P C P^{-1}$ with $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
