The transformation is defined as \( T(\mathbf{x}) = x_1\mathbf{v}_1 + x_2\mathbf{v}_2 \), where \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} \), \( \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \), and \( \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \); this is a linear combination of the vectors \( \mathbf{v}_1 \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 \).  
2. By linearity, \( T(\mathbf{x}) = x_1\mathbf{v}_1 + x_2\mathbf{v}_2 = x_1\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} + x_2\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \); this follows from the definition of matrix-vector multiplication.  
3. Combine the vectors into a single matrix multiplication: \( T(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 7 \\ 5 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} \); this is valid because matrix multiplication distributes over addition and scalar multiplication.  
4. Thus, the matrix \( A \) such that \( T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} \) is \( A = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 7 \\ 5 & -3 \end{bmatrix} \); this is verified by checking that multiplying \( A \) by \( \mathbf{x} \) yields the correct linear combination of \( \mathbf{v}_1 \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 \).
