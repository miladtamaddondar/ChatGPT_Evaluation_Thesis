1. To diagonalize $A$, we first find its eigenvalues by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $\det\begin{bmatrix}0-\lambda & 1\\-3 & 4-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$.
2. Factoring the quadratic, we get $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, so the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$; this is correct since the determinant and trace match the product and sum of the eigenvalues.
3. For $\lambda_1 = 1$, solve $(A - I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - I = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & 1\\-3 & 3\end{bmatrix}$, so $-v_1 + v_2 = 0$ and $-3v_1 + 3v_2 = 0$; both equations are multiples, so any vector of the form $\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ is an eigenvector.
4. For $\lambda_2 = 3$, solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix}-3 & 1\\-3 & 1\end{bmatrix}$, so $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$ and $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$; any vector of the form $\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}$ is an eigenvector.
5. The eigenvectors $\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$ and $\begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}$ are linearly independent, so $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$; this is correct since their determinant is $1 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot 1 = 2 \neq 0$.
6. Therefore, the basis $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$ diagonalizes $A$, so $[T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 3\end{bmatrix}$.
