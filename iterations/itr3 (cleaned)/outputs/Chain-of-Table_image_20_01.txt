To find a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\), first find all eigenvalues of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7 \end{bmatrix}\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\left(\begin{bmatrix} 0.6-\lambda & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7-\lambda \end{bmatrix}\right) = (0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - (0.3)(0.4) = 0\).
2. Expanding, \((0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = \lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\).
3. Solve the quadratic equation: \(\lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\) gives \(\lambda = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.69 - 1.2}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm 0.7}{2}\), so \(\lambda_1 = 1\), \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\).
4. We are given \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 3/7 \\ 4/7 \end{bmatrix}\) as an eigenvector for \(\lambda_1 = 1\), now find an eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) for \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\) by solving \((A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0\): \(A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix} 0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix}\).
5. Set up the equation: \(\begin{bmatrix} 0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\), which gives \(0.3x + 0.3y = 0\) and \(0.4x + 0.4y = 0\).
6. Both equations are equivalent, so \(x + y = 0 \implies y = -x\), so an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}\).
7. Thus, a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\) is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 3/7 \\ 4/7 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
