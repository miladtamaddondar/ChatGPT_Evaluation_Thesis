1. To find a \( 2 \times 2 \) matrix \( B \) such that \( AB = 0 \), we need each column of \( B \) to be in the null space of \( A \); let's first find the null space of \( A = \begin{bmatrix}3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2\end{bmatrix} \) by solving \( A\mathbf{x} = 0 \).
2. Let \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2\end{bmatrix} \), then \( 3x_1 - 6x_2 = 0 \) and \( -x_1 + 2x_2 = 0 \); both equations are equivalent, so \( x_1 = 2x_2 \).
3. The null space is all scalar multiples of \( \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} \), so any nonzero vector of this form is a valid column for \( B \); let's pick two different nonzero multiples, say \( \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} \) and \( \begin{bmatrix}4 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \). 
4. Construct \( B = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} \); verify by computing \( AB = \begin{bmatrix}3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3\cdot2 + (-6)\cdot1 & 3\cdot4 + (-6)\cdot2 \\ -1\cdot2 + 2\cdot1 & -1\cdot4 + 2\cdot2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}6 - 6 & 12 - 12 \\ -2 + 2 & -4 + 4\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \). 
5. Since \( AB \) is the zero matrix and both columns of \( B \) are nonzero and linearly dependent (as required by the null space being 1-dimensional), the solution is correct: \( B = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} \).
