1. Find the eigenvalues of \(A\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\): \(\det\begin{bmatrix}0-\lambda & 1\\-3 & 4-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0\).  
2. Factor and solve the characteristic equation: \(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda-1)(\lambda-3) = 0\), so the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = 3\).  
3. For \(\lambda_1 = 1\), solve \((A - I)\mathbf{v} = 0\): \(A - I = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & 1\\-3 & 3\end{bmatrix}\), so \(-v_1 + v_2 = 0\) and \(-3v_1 + 3v_2 = 0\), which both reduce to \(v_1 = v_2\); thus, an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}\).  
4. For \(\lambda_2 = 3\), solve \((A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0\): \(A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix}-3 & 1\\-3 & 1\end{bmatrix}\), so \(-3v_1 + v_2 = 0\) and \(-3v_1 + v_2 = 0\), which gives \(v_2 = 3v_1\); thus, an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\).  
5. The set \(\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\right\}\) is a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) since the vectors are linearly independent (the determinant of the matrix with these columns is \(1 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot 1 = 2 \neq 0\)).  
6. In this basis, the matrix of \(T\) is diagonal with the eigenvalues on the diagonal: \([T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 3\end{bmatrix}\).  
7. Final answer: A basis \(\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\3\end{bmatrix}\right\}\) diagonalizes \(A\), so \([T]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0\\0 & 3\end{bmatrix}\).
