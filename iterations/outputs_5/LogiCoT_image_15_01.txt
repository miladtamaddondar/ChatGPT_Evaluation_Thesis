1. To find \( P \) and \( C \), we first find the eigenvalues of \( A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \) by solving \( \det(A - \lambda I) = 0 \).  
2. Compute \( \det\left(\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix}\right) = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0 \).  
3. The eigenvalues are \( \lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16-20}}{2} = 2 \pm i \), which are complex conjugates, so \( C \) will be of the form \( \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ -b & a \end{pmatrix} \) with \( a = 2, b = 1 \).  
4. The eigenvector for \( \lambda = 2+i \) is found by solving \( (A - (2+i)I)\vec{v} = 0 \), i.e., \( \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}\vec{v} = 0 \).  
5. Let \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} \), then \( (-1-i)x - 2y = 0 \) and \( x + (1-i)y = 0 \); solve for \( y \) in terms of \( x \): \( x + (1-i)y = 0 \implies y = \frac{-x}{1-i} \).  
6. Rationalize the denominator: \( y = \frac{-x(1+i)}{(1-i)(1+i)} = \frac{-x(1+i)}{2} \).  
7. So the eigenvector is \( \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -\frac{1+i}{2} \end{pmatrix} \), and its complex conjugate is for \( \lambda = 2-i \).  
8. To get a real matrix \( P \), use the real and imaginary parts of \( \vec{v} \): \( \operatorname{Re}(\vec{v}) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \), \( \operatorname{Im}(\vec{v}) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \).  
9. Thus, set \( P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \), and \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \).  
10. Verify: \( A = P C P^{-1} \) by direct calculation; since \( C \) is the real canonical form for complex eigenvalues and \( P \) is built from real and imaginary parts of the eigenvector, the similarity transformation is valid.  
11. Final answer: \( P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -\frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix} \), \( C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \), and \( A = P C P^{-1} \).
