1. The problem asks us to find a vector \(\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3\) such that \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T\) where \(\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\). 
2. Write the right-hand side as an outer product: \(\mathbf{u} \mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \cdot v_1 & 1 \cdot v_2 & 1 \cdot v_3 \\ 2 \cdot v_1 & 2 \cdot v_2 & 2 \cdot v_3 \end{bmatrix}\). 
3. Set up equations by matching corresponding entries: \(1 = v_1\), \(-3 = v_2\), \(4 = v_3\), \(2 = 2v_1\), \(-6 = 2v_2\), \(8 = 2v_3\). 
4. Solve for \(v_1, v_2, v_3\): From the first row, \(v_1 = 1\), \(v_2 = -3\), \(v_3 = 4\); verify with the second row: \(2v_1 = 2 \rightarrow v_1 = 1\), \(2v_2 = -6 \rightarrow v_2 = -3\), \(2v_3 = 8 \rightarrow v_3 = 4\). 
5. All equations are consistent, so the solution is \(\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\).
