1. To diagonalize $A$, we first find its eigenvalues by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$: $\det\begin{bmatrix}0-\lambda & 1 \\ -3 & 4-\lambda\end{bmatrix} = (0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (-3)(1) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$.
2. Factoring the quadratic equation gives $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, so the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$.
3. For $\lambda_1 = 1$, solve $(A - I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - I = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & 1 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix}$, so $-v_1 + v_2 = 0$; thus, an eigenvector is $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$.
4. For $\lambda_2 = 3$, solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix}-3 & 1 \\ -3 & 1\end{bmatrix}$, so $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$; thus, an eigenvector is $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}$.
5. Therefore, a basis $\mathcal{B}$ for $\mathbb{R}^2$ such that $[T]_{\mathcal{B}}$ is diagonal is $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$.
