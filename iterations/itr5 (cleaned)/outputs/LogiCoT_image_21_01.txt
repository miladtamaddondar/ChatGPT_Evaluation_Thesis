1. We are given \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -2 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 \\ 3 & -2 & -5 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 7 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \), and we need to solve \( A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \).  
2. To determine if the solution is unique, we check if \( A \) is invertible by computing \( \det(A) \).  
3. Compute \( \det(A) = 1 \cdot (1 \cdot -5 - 6 \cdot -2) - 0 \cdot (-2 \cdot -5 - 6 \cdot 3) + (-2) \cdot (-2 \cdot -2 - 1 \cdot 3) \).  
4. Simplify: \( 1 \cdot (-5 + 12) - 0 + (-2) \cdot (4 - 3) = 1 \cdot 7 + (-2) \cdot 1 = 7 - 2 = 5 \).  
5. Since \( \det(A) = 5 \neq 0 \), \( A \) is invertible, so the solution is unique by the Invertible Matrix Theorem.  
6. The unique solution is \( \mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b} \).  
7. To find \( \mathbf{x} \), use Cramer's Rule or row reduction; here, let's use the augmented matrix and row reduction:  
8. Write the augmented matrix: \( \left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 0 & -2 & -1 \\ -2 & 1 & 6 & 7 \\ 3 & -2 & -5 & -3 \end{array}\right] \).  
9. Row 2 + 2 × Row 1: \( -2+2(1)=0, 1+2(0)=1, 6+2(-2)=2, 7+2(-1)=5 \), so Row 2 becomes \( [0, 1, 2, 5] \).  
10. Row 3 - 3 × Row 1: \( 3-3(1)=0, -2-3(0)=-2, -5-3(-2)=1, -3-3(-1)=0 \), so Row 3 becomes \( [0, -2, 1, 0] \).  
11. Row 3 + 2 × Row 2: \( 0+2(0)=0, -2+2(1)=0, 1+2(2)=5, 0+2(5)=10 \), so Row 3 becomes \( [0, 0, 5, 10] \).  
12. Back substitution: Row 3 gives \( 5x_3 = 10 \implies x_3 = 2 \).  
13. Row 2: \( x_2 + 2x_3 = 5 \implies x_2 + 2(2) = 5 \implies x_2 = 1 \).  
14. Row 1: \( x_1 - 2x_3 = -1 \implies x_1 - 2(2) = -1 \implies x_1 = 3 \).  
15. The unique solution is \( \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \), verified by invertibility and back substitution.
