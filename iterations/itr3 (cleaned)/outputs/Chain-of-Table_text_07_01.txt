1. Observe that \( Q r_1, Q r_2, \dots, Q r_p \) are the images of \( r_1, \dots, r_p \) under the linear transformation represented by \( Q \), so the matrix \(\left[ Q r_1 \; Q r_2 \; \dots \; Q r_p \right]\) has columns \( Q r_j \) for \( j = 1, \dots, p \). 
2. Note that if \( R = \left[ r_1 \; r_2 \; \dots \; r_p \right] \) is the \( n \times p \) matrix whose columns are the vectors \( r_j \), then multiplying \( Q \) (size \( m \times n \)) by \( R \) (size \( n \times p \)) gives an \( m \times p \) matrix whose \( j \)-th column is \( Q r_j \). 
3. Therefore, \(\left[ Q r_1 \; Q r_2 \; \dots \; Q r_p \right] = Q R\), where neither \( Q \) nor \( R \) is an identity matrix.
