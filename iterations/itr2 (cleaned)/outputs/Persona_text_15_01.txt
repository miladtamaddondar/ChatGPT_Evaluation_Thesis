1. First, we find the eigenvalues of $A$ by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$, so $\det\begin{pmatrix} 1-\lambda & -2 \\ 1 & 3-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$.
2. Solving $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$ gives $\lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16-20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} = 2 \pm i$.
3. The complex eigenvalue $2+i$ has an eigenvector $v$ found by solving $(A - (2+i)I)v = 0$, so $A - (2+i)I = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \\ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}$.
4. Setting $v = \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix}$, we get $(-1-i)x - 2y = 0$ and $x + (1-i)y = 0$.
5. From the first equation, $(-1-i)x = 2y \implies x = \frac{-2y}{1+i}$.
6. Rationalizing the denominator, $x = \frac{-2y}{1+i} \cdot \frac{1-i}{1-i} = \frac{-2y(1-i)}{(1+i)(1-i)} = \frac{-2y(1-i)}{1+1} = \frac{-y(1-i)}{1}$.
7. So, an eigenvector is $v = \begin{pmatrix} -1+i \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.
8. To construct $P$, we use the real and imaginary parts of $v$: $\operatorname{Re}(v) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\operatorname{Im}(v) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$, so $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.
9. The canonical form $C$ for eigenvalue $2+i$ is $C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.
10. Therefore, the answer is $P = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ such that $A = P C P^{-1}$.
