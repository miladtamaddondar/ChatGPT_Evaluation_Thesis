To find a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of \(\mathbf{v}_1\) and another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) of \(A\), we first need to find all eigenvalues of \(A\) by solving \(\det(A - \lambda I) = 0\). 2. Compute the characteristic polynomial: \(\det\left(\begin{bmatrix}0.6-\lambda & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.7-\lambda\end{bmatrix}\right) = (0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - (0.3)(0.4) = 0\). 3. Expand: \((0.6-\lambda)(0.7-\lambda) - 0.12 = 0.42 - 0.6\lambda - 0.7\lambda + \lambda^2 - 0.12 = \lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\). 4. Solve the quadratic equation: \(\lambda^2 - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0\) gives \(\lambda = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{(1.3)^2 - 4 \cdot 0.3}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{1.69 - 1.2}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm \sqrt{0.49}}{2} = \frac{1.3 \pm 0.7}{2}\). 5. Thus, the eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1\) and \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\). 6. We are given that \(\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}\) is an eigenvector, corresponding to \(\lambda_1 = 1\). 7. To find another eigenvector \(\mathbf{v}_2\) corresponding to \(\lambda_2 = 0.3\), solve \((A - 0.3I)\mathbf{v}_2 = 0\): \(A - 0.3I = \begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\). 8. Set up the equation: \(\begin{bmatrix}0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.4\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}\), which gives \(0.3x + 0.3y = 0\) and \(0.4x + 0.4y = 0\). 9. Both equations reduce to \(x + y = 0\), so \(y = -x\), so an eigenvector is \(\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix}\) (any nonzero multiple is valid). 10. Therefore, a basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\) is \(\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}3/7 \\ 4/7\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} \right\}\).
