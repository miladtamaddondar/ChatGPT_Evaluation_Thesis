1. To diagonalize $A$, we first find its eigenvalues by solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$; compute $A - \lambda I = \begin{bmatrix}0-\lambda & 1 \\ -3 & 4-\lambda\end{bmatrix}$.  
2. The determinant is $(0-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - (1)(-3) = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$; verify by expanding: $(-\lambda)(4-\lambda) + 3 = -4\lambda + \lambda^2 + 3 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3$.  
3. Solve $\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = 0$; factor to get $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3) = 0$, so eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 3$.  
4. For $\lambda_1 = 1$, solve $(A - I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - I = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & 1 \\ -3 & 3\end{bmatrix}$; set up $-v_1 + v_2 = 0$ and $-3v_1 + 3v_2 = 0$.  
5. Both equations reduce to $v_2 = v_1$, so an eigenvector is $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$; verify: $A\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}0*1 + 1*1 \\ -3*1 + 4*1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} = 1\mathbf{v}_1$.  
6. For $\lambda_2 = 3$, solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = 0$: $A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix}-3 & 1 \\ -3 & 1\end{bmatrix}$; set up $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$ and $-3v_1 + v_2 = 0$.  
7. Both equations reduce to $v_2 = 3v_1$, so an eigenvector is $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}$; verify: $A\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0*1 + 1*3 \\ -3*1 + 4*3\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3 \\ 9\end{bmatrix} = 3\mathbf{v}_2$.  
8. The eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}$ are linearly independent, so $\mathcal{B} = \left\{\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 3\end{bmatrix}\right\}$ is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $[T]_{\mathcal{B}}$ diagonal.
