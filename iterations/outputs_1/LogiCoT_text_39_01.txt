1. Recognize that $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T$ is the outer product of $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, resulting in a $2 \times 3$ matrix whose $(i,j)$ entry is $u_i v_j$. 
2. Let $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}$, so $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 v_1 & 1 v_2 & 1 v_3 \\ 2 v_1 & 2 v_2 & 2 v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix}$. 
3. Set this equal to the given matrix: $\begin{bmatrix} v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \\ 2v_1 & 2v_2 & 2v_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 4 \\ 2 & -6 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$. 
4. Equate corresponding entries: $v_1 = 1$, $v_2 = -3$, $v_3 = 4$, and verify $2v_1 = 2$, $2v_2 = -6$, $2v_3 = 8$ all hold. 
5. Conclude that $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$ is the solution, since substituting these values reproduces the given matrix.
