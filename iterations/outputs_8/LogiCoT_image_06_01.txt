1. To find a \(2 \times 2\) matrix \(B\) such that \(AB = 0\), we need each column of \(B\) to be in the null space of \(A\): \(A\mathbf{b}_1 = 0\) and \(A\mathbf{b}_2 = 0\), where \(\mathbf{b}_1\) and \(\mathbf{b}_2\) are the columns of \(B\).
2. The null space of \(A\) is found by solving \(A\mathbf{x} = 0\), i.e., \(\begin{bmatrix}3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}\).
3. Writing out the system: \(3x_1 - 6x_2 = 0\) and \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\).
4. From the first equation, \(3x_1 = 6x_2 \implies x_1 = 2x_2\); from the second equation, \(-x_1 + 2x_2 = 0 \implies x_1 = 2x_2\), which is consistent.
5. Therefore, the null space is all scalar multiples of \(\begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}\), so any nonzero vector of this form is a valid column for \(B\).
6. To use two different nonzero columns, pick two different nonzero multiples, e.g., \(\mathbf{b}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\mathbf{b}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}4 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}\).
7. Thus, \(B = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix with two different nonzero columns in the null space of \(A\).
8. Verification: \(AB = \begin{bmatrix}3 & -6 \\ -1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3 \cdot 2 + (-6) \cdot 1 & 3 \cdot 4 + (-6) \cdot 2 \\ -1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 1 & -1 \cdot 4 + 2 \cdot 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{bmatrix}\).
9. Final answer: \(B = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2\end{bmatrix}\) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix with two different nonzero columns such that \(AB = 0\).
